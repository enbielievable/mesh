Western science has taught us that nature is separate from humans, that there are only two genders and that (hetero)sexual reproduction is the norm. Queer Ecology seeks to challenge these assumptions, blurring boundaries between well-established binaries such as culture/nature, male/female, self/other, normal/abnormal etc.; oppositions that entail a hierarchy which deems the ‚Äòothered‚Äô part inferior (Plumwood, 1993). Lichens have been described as innately queer as their study requires us to think outside of rigid binaries (Griffiths, 2015). They challenge primacy of (hetero)sexual reproduction involving sexual and asexual reproductive strategies and, as a recent study suggests, it is possible that some lichen species are able to reversibly or irreversibly switch between mating types (Pizarro et al., 2019). Above all, lichens queer the notion of the individual, consisting of two or more partners ‚Äì usually a fungus and an alga - that live in symbiosis, a mutually beneficial inter-kingdom association. They represent a major actor in an ‚Äúecological mesh‚Äù where interspecies collaboration is not an anomaly. Lichens are not either/or, they are both. Photography too has been affected by binarist thinking. The concept of the negative/positive binary has long accompanied our understanding of traditional photographic practices. Historically, the negative has been described as depraved, ‚Äúan inversion of nature‚Äù (Batchen, 2020), as opposed to the positive that represents the ‚Äònormal‚Äô. Digital photography may not involve the negative/positive binary anymore, but it is essentially made up of ones and zeros. Based on a binary coding system, computers inevitably employ binarist thinking assigning everything they can into black and white boxes, while erasing all that does not fit within this notion of either/or (Bridle, 2022). Exploring these concepts, I adopted a scientific approach of photographing the lichens isolated from their environment and converted the resulting images into bitmaps, a file format that consists of merely black and white pixels. Viewing the images from afar there is an illusion of integrity and wholeness. Approaching, we realise that each image is just a matrix of black and white dots, an orderly accumulation of binary numbers. Lichens, however, do not fit in black and white boxes and their way of being is not easy to grasp. Perhaps an inversion of the ‚Äònormal‚Äô will help us visualise what Queer Ecology suggests: a world without binaries where diverse beings float in an ‚Äúopen mesh of possibilities‚Äù (Morton, 2010, p. 275).

Lichens When Swiss botanist Simon Schwendener first suspected that lichens are not an individual being in 1869, his theory was opposed by many scientists. The idea of species from two different kingdoms so closely working together in the interest of both was incomprehensible from the established perspective of taxonomy where species were considered to evolve from diverging rather than converging with each other (Sheldrake, 2021). The term ‚Äòsymbiosis‚Äô was coined a few years later by botanist and biologist Albert Bernhard Frank (1877) to name this mutually beneficial association between a fungus and an alga. In the late 1960‚Äôs biologist Lynn Margulis however expanded the theory of symbiosis to the whole world explaining that the story of evolution is not about competition but about networking and collaboration (1999), a hypothesis increasingly gaining proponents. Today, whilst it is accepted that lichens do live in symbiosis, they still make us question where the boundaries of a single organism begin and end (Griffiths, 2015). Furthermore, recent studies have shown that some lichens involve even more than just two symbiotic partners and that there is no ‚Äòbasic set‚Äô of partners that applies to all lichens (Sheldrake, 2021). The study of lichens continues raising questions as they don‚Äôt seem to fit within the categories constructed by Western science. As Sheldrake explains once the ‚Äòdual hypothesis‚Äô of lichens was established ‚Äúevolution could no longer be thought of solely in terms of competition and conflict‚Äù (2021, p. 77). In addition, the ecological importance of lichens is immense from many aspects. They are the first to establish themselves in harsh conditions preparing the soil for the plants that will follow (Sheldrake, 2021). They are involved in complex naturalcultural relationships with humans, non-human animals and plants, serving as food, shelter, camouflage, decoration, medicine and many more (Griffiths, 2015). Lichens represent a major actor in an ‚Äúecological mesh‚Äù where interspecies collaboration is not an anomaly. Last but not least, along with their symbiotic lifestyle they also demonstrate sexual and asexual reproductive strategies challenging the primacy of (hetero)sexual reproduction (Griffiths, 2015). Looking at lichens and recognising their queer way of being enables us to let go of the preconceived categories we learnt to ascribe to everything. Binary categories don‚Äôt help us understand the world better, rather they conceal a huge rainbow of diversity.
